There are thousands of AI tool reviews online. Most of them are useless.

Not because the writers are incompetent — but because the incentives are wrong. A site that earns commission from every tool it recommends has a structural reason to say everything is great. A writer who got a free trial for three days has no idea how the tool performs at scale. And a listicle written six months ago is already outdated in a market that moves this fast.

So how do you actually evaluate an AI tool in 2026?

## The three questions that matter

**1. Does it solve a specific problem, or is it trying to solve everything?**

The best AI tools do one thing well. The worst ones are feature factories — they add capabilities faster than they can make any of them reliable. When a tool claims to replace your entire workflow, that's usually a red flag, not a selling point.

**2. What does the pricing look like after the free trial?**

Many AI tools offer generous free tiers to get you hooked, then wall off the features you actually need behind expensive plans. Before committing to any tool, check the paid tier pricing and calculate what you'd actually pay per month if you used it seriously.

**3. Is there a realistic exit strategy?**

If you build your workflow around a tool and it gets acquired, pivots, or shuts down — what happens? The best tools use standard file formats and let you export your data cleanly. The worst ones lock you in.
